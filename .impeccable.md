## Design Context

### Users

IT administrators at **Embedded Silicon** — a small, experienced team who live in this tool daily. Power users who need to track hardware assets, manage storeroom inventory, and audit transactions. They are not entry-level users; they are precision-oriented people who get frustrated by wasted motion, unclear affordances, and visual noise. The interface should feel like a tool they trust, not a product they have to navigate around.

### Brand Personality

**Precise. Calm. Polished.**

Like a well-calibrated instrument. Nothing decorative — every element earns its place by serving the data. Not cold or sterile, but definitively professional. The emotional goal is quiet confidence: users should feel that the tool has everything under control so they can focus on the work.

### Aesthetic Direction

**Keep and refine the current layered depth system.**

The defining visual signature of Pandora is its three-layer depth model:
1. **Dark background** — `bg-auth.jpg`, nearly black, always present behind everything
2. **Blue glass sidebar** — `rgba(46, 124, 253, 0.88)` with `backdrop-filter: blur(4px)`, the primary navigation surface
3. **White/light content panels** — crisp, high-contrast working surface where data lives

This layering creates a spatial hierarchy that feels intentional and distinctive — preserve it in all future work. Do not flatten it into a purely dark or purely light UI.

Refinement targets:
- Tighter internal spacing on stat cards and table rows (no wasted padding)
- More deliberate use of the orange accent (`#fc9c2d`) — reserve it for genuine urgency (warnings, archive badge, overdue items)
- Cyan accent (`#2dfcf9`) should be used sparingly if at all — it risks looking garish against the white content areas
- Typography hierarchy within content panels needs more contrast between heading and body sizes

### Design Principles

1. **Precision over decoration** — Every element must serve the data or the user's task. Decorative chrome, placeholder illustrations, or visual flourishes that don't carry information should be removed.

2. **Depth through layers** — The dark → glass sidebar → white content layering is the product's visual identity. All future surfaces should slot into one of these three layers. Never add a fourth layer (no card-in-card, no floating panels on top of white panels).

3. **Calm by default, urgent when needed** — The interface should be quiet. The blue primary anchors navigation and primary actions. Orange is reserved for genuine system signals (pending, overdue, needs attention). Red is reserved for destructive actions only.

4. **Density without fatigue** — IT admins need to scan many rows quickly. Tables should use tight, consistent row heights with clear visual separation. No excessive padding. Hierarchy through typography weight and color, not size alone.

5. **Trustworthy at first glance** — Polish is visible at arm's length: consistent border radii, exact alignment, no orphaned elements. Every interactive surface should give immediate, legible feedback. Hover states, active states, and focus rings are required, not optional.
